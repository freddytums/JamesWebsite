SiSolar

Sun Tracking Solar Panel

This project, SiSolar, was completed as my team's UTDallas Senior Design Project from 2019-2020.

Our mission was to develop a solar energy harnessing system which maintains maximum efficiency by tracking the sun throughout the day. Additional design requirements were to minimize the system's power consumption as well as avoiding the use of internet or external input.

The project is comprised of two assemblies: the Sun Angle Detector Assembly, and the Solar Panel Orienter Assembly.

The Sun Angle Detector Assembly is a light sensing circuit board that is illuminated with a pinhole projection of the sky. A micro-controller takes the circuit board's output, and triangulates the position of the sun.

The Solar Panel Orienter Assembly is a two axis motor that pitches and yaws the affixed solar panel.

Below you can find more in depth information about this project!

The project is comprised of two assemblies: the Sun Angle Detector Assembly, and the Solar Panel Orienter Assembly.

The Sun Angle Detector is PCB covered by a shell. The shell includes a pinhole that introduces a projection of the sun onto the PCB which is populated with grid of LEDs. When each of the LEDs is illuminated by the projection of the sun, they generate voltages on the grid which correspond to a coordinate that the master microcontroller is able to determine.

The Solar Panel Orienter is relayed the current position from the Sun Angle Detectors controller. The orienters controller then pitches and yaws the Solar Panel to face directly at the sun.

This concept is expandable such that an array of Solar Panel Orienters could be controlled by a single Sun Angle Detector. The concept is also modular, and easily allows for variations of the Solar Panel Orienter mechanism.

This concept also functions without the use of the internet or any external data source, providing the capability to be used off-grid.

The mechanism for orienting the solar panel consists of a few moving components. The Solar Panel is mounted to a folding frame which is controlled by a linear actuator that pitches the panel. The frame is mounted onto an azimuth bearing, and then driven by a motor that rotates the entire frame.

The mechanism is designed to hold position if power is removed. The linear actuator and the azimuth torqueing motor both were selected for having high gear ratios to enable the system to both withstand and overcome wind. The systemw is designed to operate on 12V. All electrical components are housed on top of the rotating layer, beneath the solar panel for at the least shade and then some minor protection from weather.
